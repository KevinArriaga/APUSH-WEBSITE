Important Events In Black History

1787

⅗ Compromise- A part of the U.S. Constitutional Convention that stated that a slave’s vote would count ⅗ that of a free man’s

1807

Slave Trade gets abolished- U.S. follows European power’s example and finally abolishes Atlantic slave trade. Slaves already in the U.S. however are still legally property

1820

Missouri Compromise- Law passed by congress that stated that any new state north of the 36th parallel would be considered a “free” state, while any new state south of that line would be considered a “slave” state

1837

Nat Turner’s Slave Rebellion- About 60 white people killed. 56 slaves were executed or killed as a result of the conflict

1850

Fugitive Slave Act- A law that allowed for escaped slaves in the North to be recaptured by their owners. Owners were allowed to place bounties to aid the return of escaped slaves

1854

Kansas-Nebraska Act- The law that overturns the Missouri Compromise and puts in place the policy of popular sovereignty for the entry of new states. State had to vote on whether they would become a slave or free state. Led to a series of conflicts in newer states, namely Kansas, over slavery. Violence dubbed, “Bleeding Kansas”

1857

Dred Scott v. Sanford- A supreme court case that ruled in favor of Southern ideals and stated that Africans were to be considered as property, and that Scott wasn’t even supposed to be there in the first place

1859

Raid On Harper’s Ferry- An attack of an arsenal at Harper’s Ferry led by John Brown, a white radical abolitionist who sought to lead an armed slave rebellion. He failed, but increased North and South relations, and was made a martyr for the cause of the slaves

1861

Beginning of Civil War- The secession of South Carolina, and eventually most of the other Southern states led to the beginning of the Civil War, or “The War of Northern Aggression” as named by some Southerners

1863

Emancipation Proclamation- An address by Abraham Lincoln stating that if the Southern states still refused to rejoin the Union, all slaves in said rebelling states would be considered free

1865-1870

Passing of 13th, 14th, and 15th Amendments- the end of the Civil War and the passing of these amendments were a turning point in African-American history, as they gave basic human rights to blacks, such as the complete abolition of slavery,and allowing them to vote

1877

Jim Crow Laws- Shortly after the abrupt end of Lincoln’s presidency, the new president, Andrew Johnson, was much more lax and turned a blind eye to the passing of segregation laws that restricted the newly given rights of African-Americans

1896

Plessy v. Ferguson- A supreme court case that allowed for the segregation of white and “colored” communities in American society

1920

Harlem Renaissance- Large Migration of African-Americans North in search of jobs leads to great increase of awareness of black culture, and cultural pride. Helps shape American media

1936

Jesse Owens Olympic Victories- African American track and field athlete who won 4 gold medals in the 1936 Berlin Olympics, exemplifying African-American pride

1947

Jackie Robinson Enters MLB- Jackie Robinson becomes the first African-American to play professional baseball, becomes a symbol for desegregation

1948

Desegregation of Military- Ordered by President Harry S. Truman after World War 2, the U.S. military became desegregated

1950

First African-Americans in NBA- The addition of Chuck Cooper, Nat Clifton, and Earl Lloyd marks the first time African-Americans were drafted to the NBA

1954

Brown v. Board of Education- Overturned the Plessy v. Ferguson supreme court decision and eventually desegregated all facilities in the U.S

1963

“I Have A Dream” Speech- Famous speech made by Martin Luther KIng Jr. that inspired people to help fight for civil rights

1964

Civil Rights Act- Protected the rights of African-Americans in the U.S., caused by multiple movements and the efforts of multiple activists, such as Malcolm X, Martin Luther King Jr., and many others

1965

Voting Rights Act- Passed by Lyndon B. Johnson to further enforce and protect the voting rights of African-Americans, and banned use of restrictive voting, such as poll taxes

2008

First Black President- Barack Obama becomes the first African-American president in American history, and it only took about 44 tries. Taking bets on how long it’ll take for an Asian one
